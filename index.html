Personal Reflection

Who Am I? Where Do I Come From?

Growing up in Envigado, in the metropolitan area of Medellín, I was surrounded by a diverse community — a city where stories from all corners of Antioquia converge. From an early age, I felt a deep curiosity not only about the stars above but also about the human histories beneath them: the people who shaped my character, my values, and my way of seeing the world.

My family has been a constant anchor. Among those most present in my life is my grandmother, whose daily wellbeing I actively follow and support — including managing her nutrition and health routines. This role has taught me that science and care are not opposites; they are two sides of the same human coin.

A Story Worth Telling

The Astronomy of Everyday Things

One of the first narratives I wrote in the workshop was about the night I realised that physics and family were not so different. I was helping my grandmother organise her weekly meals — tracking calories, managing schedules — and I realised I was doing the same mental operation I do in a Lagrangian mechanics problem: identifying variables, finding equilibrium, predicting outcomes.

The people I admire most, including my grandmother, have always been quiet agents of order in a disordered world. This portfolio is, in part, a tribute to those people to know — the ones whose stories rarely appear in textbooks.

Trekking · Antioquia

Walking the Hidden Paths of Envigado & La Doctora

The hills and forests of Antioquia offer some of the most striking natural corridors in Colombia. My exploration diary includes trails around La Doctora, waterfalls tucked between ridges, and cloud-forest paths that change colour with the altitude. Each walk is documented — elevation, duration, what I found — with the same systematic attention I give to a physics problem set.

Trekking is not a passive activity. It demands reading terrain, managing energy, and being present in a way that a city rarely allows. For me, it is also the best way to find dark-sky sites — locations far enough from Medellín's light pollution to see the Milky Way with the naked eye.

Astrophotography

Capturing the Night Sky from the Hills of Envigado

With a basic camera and a tripod, the hilltops above Envigado become observatories. I photograph star trails, constellations, and whenever conditions allow, the diffuse band of the Milky Way. Every image is also a data point: date, time, sky conditions, focal length, ISO, and exposure time are recorded.

This practice sits at the intersection of all my interests — the patience of a swimmer, the precision of a physicist, the narrative instinct of a writer, and the wonder of a person who simply loves to walk into the dark and look up.

Astronomy Project

Apophis 2029 & the N-Body Problem

In 2029, the asteroid 99942 Apophis will make one of the closest approaches to Earth ever recorded for an object of its size. My current research project models its orbital trajectory using the N-body gravitational problem — a system of differential equations that describes how multiple massive bodies influence each other's motion simultaneously.

The project uses Lagrangian mechanics and numerical integration in Python. The goal is to refine trajectory predictions and understand the limits of classical determinism at the edge of a gravitational encounter. It is also an exercise in what physicists call sensitivity to initial conditions — a small error in early measurements can produce vastly different outcomes over time.

Philosophy of Method

Why Science Is an Agent of Change

The projects above share a common logic: take a complex, apparently chaotic system — an asteroid's path, a personal budget, a policy document — and find the underlying structure that makes it predictable, manageable, and improvable.

This is what it means, for me, to be an agent of change: not grand gestures, but the patient application of good tools to real problems. A well-built Python script that tracks your spending is as much an act of agency as publishing a paper. Both are attempts to replace noise with signal.

My daily discipline — structured morning and afternoon training in swimming, followed by focused research hours — reflects the same belief: that consistent, structured effort, applied over time, produces results that feel impossible in the short term and inevitable in retrospect.
